01

What is a Directional Overcurrent Relay?

An overcurrent relay (OC) simply trips when current exceeds a set threshold — like a circuit breaker. But on a meshed network (where power can flow from two directions), knowing that current is high is not enough. You also need to know which direction the fault current is flowing.

A Directional Overcurrent (DOC) relay adds a directional element to the overcurrent element. It compares the angle of the fault current against a reference voltage phasor. Only when the current is flowing in the forward direction AND exceeds the threshold will the relay trip.

💡

Simple analogy: Imagine a one-way traffic sensor. It counts cars (overcurrent), but only triggers an alarm if they're travelling the wrong way (directionality). A DOC relay is that sensor — for electrical fault current.

DOC relays follow IEC 60255-151 which defines the operating characteristics, torque equation, and timing curves for directional protection elements.

02

The Problem — Why Direction Matters

Consider a substation bus fed from two sources — Source A on the left and Source B on the right. A feeder goes out from the middle of the bus.

When a fault occurs on the feeder, both sources pump current into the fault. The relay on the feeder should see this as a forward fault and trip. But the relay on Source B sees the same high current — however that current is actually flowing away from the fault (toward Source A). It should not trip.

⚠️

A plain overcurrent relay cannot distinguish these two cases. It sees high current in both relays and trips both — blacking out a healthy source. The directional element solves this by checking the current angle against a polarising voltage reference.

03

Phasors — Quick Primer

In AC systems, voltages and currents are sinusoidal. We represent them as phasors — rotating arrows where the length is the magnitude and the angle is the phase position relative to a reference.

Our three-phase system voltages at the relay secondary (VT ratio gives 63.51 V) are:

System Voltages
VR = 63.51 V ∠ 0° (Reference — R phase, on the horizontal axis)
VY = 63.51 V ∠ 240° (Y phase, lags VR by 120°)
VB = 63.51 V ∠ 120° (B phase, lags VR by 240°)
Three-phase voltage phasors at relay secondary · 63.51 V each · 120° apart
✅

These three phasors are always fixed in our tool. They are the healthy system voltages measured at the relay terminals. They rotate together at 50 Hz, but their relative angles — 0°, 120°, 240° — never change under normal conditions.

04

Cross-Polarisation — Phase-to-Phase Faults

The challenge during a fault

When a phase-to-phase fault occurs (e.g. R–Y short circuit), the faulted phase voltage collapses toward zero. If we used the faulted phase voltage as the reference for our directional element, the reference itself would disappear — the relay would have nothing to compare against.

⚠️

Problem: You cannot use the voltage of a faulted phase as a reference — it collapses during the fault.

The solution: use the healthy phase voltages

For a fault on phase R, phases Y and B are unaffected. We subtract them to get a cross-polarising voltage Vyb = Vy − Vb. This voltage stays healthy and has a well-defined angle we can use as our reference.

1
Identify the healthy phases
For R-phase fault → healthy phases are Y and B. For Y-phase fault → healthy phases are R and B. For B-phase fault → healthy phases are R and Y.
2
Form the polarising voltage (phasor subtraction)
Subtract the two healthy phasors: Vpol = V(leading healthy) − V(lagging healthy). This gives a resultant phasor at a defined angle.
3
Apply the MTA offset
Add the relay's characteristic angle (fault angle, typically 45°) to the reference angle. This is the Maximum Torque Angle — the relay operates at peak sensitivity at this angle.

Worked example — R-phase fault

Step-by-step · R-Phase Fault
Given: VY = 63.51 V ∠240°, VB = 63.51 V ∠120°
Step 1 · Convert to rectangular form:
VY = 63.51×cos(240°) + j·63.51×sin(240°)
= 63.51×(−0.5) + j·63.51×(−0.866)
= −31.755 − j54.99
VB = 63.51×cos(120°) + j·63.51×sin(120°)
= 63.51×(−0.5) + j·63.51×(+0.866)
= −31.755 + j54.99
Step 2 · Subtract (VY − VB):
Vyb = (−31.755 − j54.99) − (−31.755 + j54.99)
= 0 − j109.98
= 110 V ∠270° (straight down on phasor diagram)
Step 3 · Add fault angle (MTA offset = 45°):
MTA = 270° + 45° = 315°
✓ R-phase fault → Polarising reference = 270° → MTA = 315°

All three phases — cross-polarisation summary

R-Phase Fault
Vpol = Vy − Vb
= 63.51∠240° − 63.51∠120°
= 110 V ∠270°
MTA = 270° + 45° = 315°
Y-Phase Fault
Vpol = Vr − Vb
= 63.51∠0° − 63.51∠120°
= 110 V ∠330° → ref 150°
MTA = 150° + 45° = 195°
B-Phase Fault
Vpol = Vr − Vy
= 63.51∠0° − 63.51∠240°
= 110 V ∠30°
MTA = 30° + 45° = 75°
📐

Notice the cross-polarising voltage magnitude is always √3 × Vphase = √3 × 63.51 ≈ 110 V. This is because subtracting two phasors 120° apart always yields a resultant √3 times larger. This is identical to the line voltage.

This method is defined under IEC 60255-151 §5.4 Cross-Polarisation as the standard approach for phase-to-phase directional elements.

05

Zero-Sequence Polarisation — Earth Faults

Why earth faults are different

An earth fault (single-phase to ground) involves current flowing through the earth path. The fault current contains a large zero-sequence component — a component that is identical in all three phases (same magnitude, same angle).

For earth faults, cross-polarisation doesn't apply because the fault involves ground, not a phase-to-phase short. Instead, we use the zero-sequence voltage V₀ as the polarising reference.

🌍

Zero-sequence voltage (V₀) only appears when there is an earth fault. Under balanced conditions V₀ = 0. During an earth fault, V₀ rises sharply — making it an ideal, unambiguous polarising signal for earth fault direction.

How V₀ relates to phase voltages

The zero-sequence voltage is the average of the three phase voltages. During a single-phase earth fault on phase R, the faulted phase voltage collapses and V₀ appears at an angle close to the faulted phase angle:

Zero-Sequence Voltage
V₀ = (VR + VY + VB) / 3
Under normal (balanced) conditions:
V₀ = (63.51∠0° + 63.51∠240° + 63.51∠120°) / 3 = 0 ✓
During R-phase earth fault (VR collapses):
V₀ ≈ reference angle aligned with the faulted phase = 0°
✓ V₀ reference angle = faulted phase angle

MTA offset for earth fault

For earth fault elements, the relay characteristic angle is typically set to −45° (or equivalently +315°). This accounts for the angle between the zero-sequence current and voltage in a typical earthed network, ensuring maximum relay torque aligns with the actual fault current direction.

Earth Fault MTA Calculation
General formula:
MTA = V₀ reference angle + (−45°)
= Faulted phase angle − 45°
R-phase earth fault: MTA = 0° + (−45°) = −45° = 315°
Y-phase earth fault: MTA = 240° + (−45°) = 195°
B-phase earth fault: MTA = 120° + (−45°) = 75°
✓ Same MTA values as cross-polarisation — but derived differently
🔍

You'll notice the final MTA values are the same (315°, 195°, 75°) for both methods at their respective phases. This is intentional — relay engineers choose fault angles and offsets so that the operating zones remain consistent across fault types, making coordination simpler.

Zero-sequence polarisation is standardised under IEC 60255-151 §5.5 Zero-Sequence and requires the relay to have access to the residual voltage connection VR + VY + VB from a VT open-delta winding or neutral point measurement.

06

MTA, Operating Zone & Blind Zone

Maximum Torque Angle (MTA)

The MTA is the angle at which the relay produces its maximum operating torque — meaning it is most sensitive and will trip fastest. It is the direction of the reference phasor after adding the fault characteristic angle.

General MTA Formula
MTA = Polarising reference angle + Fault characteristic angle (RCA)
Where RCA (Relay Characteristic Angle) is typically:
Phase-to-phase elements: RCA = +45° (common setting)
Earth fault elements: RCA = −45° (common setting)

Operating zone — the ±90° rule

The relay operates (trips) when the fault current phasor falls within ±90° of the MTA. This 180° sector is the operating zone. The opposite 180° is the restraint zone — relay will NOT trip even if current exceeds the threshold.

Operating Zone Boundaries
Operating zone: from (MTA − 90°) to (MTA + 90°)
Restraint zone: from (MTA + 90°) to (MTA − 90°) [other half]
Example — R-phase fault, MTA = 315°:
Lower boundary = 315° − 90° = 225°
Upper boundary = 315° + 90° = 405° = 45°
✓ Operating zone: 225° → 315° → 45° (sweeping through 315°)

Blind zone — the uncertainty region

At the exact boundary (MTA ± 90°), the relay torque is zero. In practice, real-world noise, CT/VT errors, and system disturbances mean that a fault current very close to the boundary may or may not operate the relay reliably.

A blind zone of typically 5° is defined inside the operating zone, adjacent to each boundary. In this zone, the relay might operate, but is not guaranteed to operate reliably.

⚠️

The blind zone sits entirely within the operating region — touching the boundary and extending 5° inward toward the MTA. It does not overlap into the restraint zone. Fault currents falling in the blind zone are uncertain — the relay may or may not trip.

Blind Zone Boundaries (5°)
Upper boundary blind zone: (MTA + 90° − 5°) to (MTA + 90°)
Lower boundary blind zone: (MTA − 90°) to (MTA − 90° + 5°)
Example — MTA = 315°:
Upper blind zone: 40° to 45° [just inside operating, near 45° boundary]
Lower blind zone: 225° to 230° [just inside operating, near 225° boundary]
